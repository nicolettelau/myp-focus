The Purpose of Provocation

  • Provocations activate prior knowledge, spark curiosity, and create conceptual entry points
  • They are a core part of IB MYP Approaches to Teaching — specifically inquiry-based and conceptually focused learning

Designing a Provocation

⏱ 8 minutes
"A focus on higher-order thinking skills gives students opportunities to explore their expanding concerns and their growing awareness of themselves and the world in ways that develop sound judgment." — MYP: From Principles into Practice (IB, 2014)
The MYP Unit Planner's Inquiry section asks teachers to establish:
  • What is the purpose of the unit?
  • What questions will drive the inquiry?
  • How will you provoke curiosity and surface prior knowledge?
— MYP Unit Planner: Inquiry — Establishing the Purpose of the Unit
A provocation is not simply a warm-up activity. It is the inquiry entry point built into our unit/lesson — the moment we activate higher-order thinking and make students genuinely want to find out more.

✅ A Provocation Should:

  • Connect to the Concepts of the unit
  • Be open-ended — no single right answer
  • Create cognitive dissonance or emotional engagement
  • Be accessible to all learners regardless of prior knowledge
  • Lead naturally into the Statement of Inquiry

🗂 Types of Provocations:
